# clawin-research-analysis

Use this skill when the role conducts market research, sector analysis, monitoring, screening, scenario planning, data review, or analytical reporting. The core responsibility is disciplined reasoning: gather relevant evidence, assess its reliability, separate observation from inference, and communicate conclusions with the right level of confidence.

Start by defining the analytical question. Are you monitoring events, comparing peers, screening opportunities, studying a market, analyzing a sector theme, supporting portfolio research, or testing scenarios? Clarify the decision being supported, the time horizon, the unit of analysis, and any constraints on evidence quality. If the question is broad, narrow it into hypotheses or research sub-questions before proceeding.

## Core methods

Use a structured research flow:
1. **Question framing**: Define the objective, scope, and what “good enough” evidence looks like.
2. **Source plan**: Identify primary and secondary sources, required data points, and refresh cadence.
3. **Collection**: Gather data systematically with timestamps, source notes, and inclusion criteria.
4. **Validation**: Check consistency across sources, identify gaps, and flag weak or stale evidence.
5. **Analysis**: Compare, segment, trend, benchmark, or model scenarios as needed.
6. **Synthesis**: State conclusions, confidence level, assumptions, and what to monitor next.

Useful methods include hypothesis trees, peer benchmarking, event timelines, scenario/stress grids, sensitivity tables, trend decomposition, and source triangulation. For monitoring work, define triggers, thresholds, and watchlist logic. For scenario analysis, list assumptions explicitly and show what changes under different cases. For screening or comparison, use consistent criteria and avoid quietly changing the rubric between subjects.

## Working rules

- Keep a clear distinction between fact, estimate, and interpretation.
- Cite source type and freshness where relevant.
- Prefer triangulation over single-source claims.
- State uncertainty rather than hiding it.
- Make analytical assumptions inspectable.

## Deliverables

Typical outputs include:
- Research brief or market scan
- Monitoring digest or event summary
- Peer comparison table or screening memo
- Scenario analysis with assumptions and implications
- Sector or thematic insight note
- Source register or evidence log
- Executive summary with confidence levels

## Output format

A good analytical output includes:
- **Question / decision supported**
- **Scope and methodology**
- **Sources and evidence quality**
- **Key findings**
- **Comparisons / scenarios / trends**
- **Implications**
- **Confidence, assumptions, and open questions**

Use tables for comparisons, event tracking, scenario outcomes, and source reliability notes.

## Common pitfalls

- Starting collection before the question is sharply defined
- Treating outdated or secondhand material as current evidence
- Mixing observations and conclusions in the same sentence
- Cherry-picking data that supports the preferred answer
- Comparing entities with inconsistent criteria or time windows
- Presenting scenario outputs without assumptions or sensitivity notes

## Quality checks

Before finalizing:
- Confirm the question, scope, and method are explicit.
- Check that conclusions are proportional to the evidence strength.
- Verify source freshness and note limitations.
- Make comparisons and scenarios internally consistent.
- State assumptions, confidence, and open questions clearly.
- Ensure a reader can distinguish what is known, inferred, and still uncertain.
